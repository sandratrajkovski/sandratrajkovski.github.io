Detecting Lead Contamination with Spectrophotometry

Team project · CSULB

Overview

Long Beach apartment complexes need an accurate, low-cost way to detect lead contamination in water before it reaches residents. Commercial spectrophotometers are effective but expensive, so our team built a low-cost, 3D-printed spectrophotometer using an Arduino, a phototransistor, and swappable LED light sources to test whether an accessible design could still deliver reliable readings.

Hypothesis

  • A yellow LED would detect contamination levels more effectively than a green LED
  • Adding a 3D-printed phototransistor/LED cover and cuvette holder would improve detection consistency by blocking outside light

Design & Method

Green dye at five concentrations simulated lead contamination levels in water samples. Each concentration was measured under a yellow LED and a green LED, both with and without a 3D-printed enclosure (cuvette holder + light-blocking box, designed in SolidWorks and iterated three times to fix fit and light-leak issues). An Arduino Uno circuit converted phototransistor readings to voltage and printed a corresponding safety message.

Results

The 3D-printed enclosure meaningfully improved measurement consistency for both LEDs compared to the open-air setup. The green LED with the enclosure held the most consistent readings overall, especially at higher contamination levels. The yellow LED was slightly less consistent overall, but it caught low-concentration contamination earlier, which is arguably the more important signal for catching lead problems before they get worse.

Conclusion & Next Steps

Given that early detection matters most for preventing lead poisoning, the team's recommendation is to move forward with the yellow LED, accepting a modest tradeoff in high-concentration consistency for better sensitivity at the low end. Future iterations would use sturdier, less light-permeable materials for the enclosure and pursue pilot implementation in actual apartment complex water testing.

Swimming and Feeding Characteristics of Sand Dollar Larvae via Aquavert

University Honors Program Thesis

Overview

Larval Pacific sand dollars (Dendraster excentricus) rely on chemosensing to locate food and avoid predators during their earliest life stages, but their vertical swimming and feeding behavior in response to food gradients is hard to study in the field. This thesis used a custom microfluidic imaging platform, Aquavert, to observe how low-fed sand dollar larvae respond to algae concentration gradients across their first 14 days post-fertilization.

Method

  • Spawned and cultured Dendraster excentricus larvae from adults collected in San Pedro, CA, feeding them unicellular red algae on a controlled low-feeding schedule
  • Fabricated Y-shaped microfluidic chips from laser-cut PMMA molds and PDMS, plasma-bonded to glass slides, to create a two-inlet flow boundary between seawater and algae
  • Built Aquavert, a custom vertical imaging system combining a 2x objective, tube lens, and 3D-printed camera and chip-holder mounts, to record larval position relative to the flow boundary
  • Ran paired trials varying which inlet carried algae (top vs. bottom) and algae concentration (low vs. high) across days 1–14 post-fertilization

Results

Larvae consistently oriented toward and remained near the algae inlet across trials, regardless of whether it was positioned on the top or bottom channel. Flow rate mattered: rates at or below 1.5 mL/hr let larvae stay within the chip and feed comfortably, while higher flow rates gave a clearer visual of the algae boundary but flushed larvae out of the imaging area.

Why it matters

This was hands-on validation that a low-cost, custom-fabricated microfluidic and imaging system can capture real chemosensory behavior in marine larvae. The fabrication skills involved (laser cutting, PDMS casting, plasma bonding, 3D-printed fixtures) carry directly into device prototyping in medtech.

Integration and Optimization of Virtual Reality in EEG-Based Brain-Computer Interfaces

Team project · CSULB

Overview

Brain-computer interfaces that rely on steady-state visually evoked potentials (SSVEP, a neural response to a flickering visual stimulus at a specific frequency) typically present that stimulus on a laptop screen. This project tested whether presenting the same stimulus inside a fully immersive VR environment would produce a stronger, more usable signal, since VR is known to improve patient engagement in other clinical contexts.

Method

  • Recorded EEG from 2 subjects using a standard 19-electrode OpenBCI cap, focusing on parietal, temporal, and occipital electrodes (strongest visual response region)
  • Presented flickering stimuli at 13Hz, 15Hz, and 17Hz, each for 5-minute segments (10 trials per frequency, plus a relaxed baseline), once in VR and once on a laptop
  • Preprocessed signals with common average referencing, bandpass filtering, and whitening, then computed normalized target-to-non-target frequency power ratios per trial

Results

SSVEP power was significantly higher in the VR condition than on the laptop at all three stimulus frequencies (p < 0.05 for 13Hz, 15Hz, and 17Hz). Topographic maps showed the O1 electrode (lower-left occipital site) consistently produced the strongest response across both conditions and all three frequencies.

Conclusion

VR turned out to be a viable, and in this case stronger, stimulus platform for SSVEP-based brain-computer interfaces, which supports future use in therapeutic and rehabilitation applications. Planned follow-up work includes a machine learning pipeline for automated signal classification and live data processing.

Low-Cost ASL Translation Glove

Team project · CSULB · Total build cost: $58.71

Overview

Roughly 11 million people in the U.S. are deaf or hard of hearing, but only around 500,000 people are fluent in ASL. That gap leaves many deaf individuals dependent on a human interpreter, especially in emergencies. This project built a wearable glove that interprets ASL fingerspelling into English text in real time through a companion smartphone app, at a fraction of the cost of commercial sign-language translation gloves (about $3,300 retail).

Bill of Materials

PartQtyCost
Flex sensors4$33.35
MPU-6050 accelerometer1$9.99
HC-05 Bluetooth 2.01$9.99
Arduino Uno kit1$27.90
Glove1$1.00
9V battery1$4.50

Design & Build

  • Four flex sensors (one per finger, excluding the thumb) change resistance as they bend, letting the Arduino read finger position as an analog signal
  • An MPU-6050 accelerometer captured hand orientation across 3 axes to help distinguish gestures that look similar in finger position alone
  • Arduino firmware compared live sensor readings against reference values for each letter (A–Z) within a tolerance window, then sent a matched letter over HC-05 Bluetooth
  • A custom MIT App Inventor app received the Bluetooth signal and displayed the translated text live

Demo

Challenges & Limitations

  • The Arduino Uno didn't have enough analog input pins for all 5 sensors, so the final build used only 4 (thumb excluded)
  • The wired breadboard setup was fragile and limited hand mobility while signing
  • Soldering wires directly to the flex sensors was failure-prone
  • The HC-05 Bluetooth module had recurring connectivity issues that made repeat demos harder

Future Steps

Planned next steps include iOS support, a pre-established word bank, text-to-speech output for the non-ASL speaker, an enclosed compartment for the electronics, and a second glove synced to the first for two-handed signs.

Predicting Coma Recovery by Applying Human-Interpretable Machine Learning Models to Brain Signals

MEng Capstone, in collaboration with UCSF Health

The Problem

Subjective coma prognoses can lead to premature withdrawal of life support. Of an estimated 255,000 annual coma deaths from withdrawn life support, over 48,000 may be preventable: cases where a patient showed signs of recovery after doctors determined, without objective evidence, that recovery was unlikely.

Pipeline

  • Source localization: CHAMPAGNE algorithm applied to raw EEG to localize neural activity to specific brain regions
  • Atlas mapping: Activity mapped onto 86 Desikan-Killiany atlas regions of interest (ROIs)
  • Feature extraction: Amplitude, relative band power, and other features computed across 1,275 total feature dimensions per patient
  • Prototype classification: ProtoPNet applied to identify interpretable EEG pattern prototypes tied to outcome classes
  • Trajectory prediction: Dynamic models (as opposed to static survival/death predictions) project how a patient's recovery probability develops over time
  • Clinical translation: Findings mocked into a bedside tablet dashboard combining outcome predictions, brain signal strength, source localization, and raw EEG

Data & Results

The model was trained and evaluated on a subset of roughly 1,000 patients across 7 hospitals in 3 countries, using 1,275 feature dimensions per patient. It achieved 93% AUC-ROC, 83% sensitivity, and 88% specificity in predicting good neurological recovery. The ProtoPNet and 3D trajectory-embedding stages provide interpretable, clinician-facing explanations for each prediction, rather than a black-box score.

Why It Matters

Multi-modal interpretability gives clinicians objective, evidence-based predictions instead of relying solely on subjective judgment, which directly addresses the premature-withdrawal problem the project set out to solve. A manuscript based on this work is in the works for peer-reviewed publication.
